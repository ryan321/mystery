## Disturbance

**Mr. Henry Cole** (middle-aged) dies at Greymoor House in what looks like a solitary accident: a fall, equipment failure, or misfortune while going about **his own** business (his route, his keys, his drink, his workshop, his usual evening path). No struggle with an attacker. Everyone “knows” Mrs. Lillian Voss — who uses a wheelchair — could not have attacked him, could not have reached what killed him, and had no way to move fast or arrange a fight. You must prove the accident was built.

## Stakes

- Death ruled misfortune; killer keeps the house narrative.  
- Wheelchair logic blocks suspicion.  
- Henry dies because of what he knew / was about to do — not random cruelty.

## Sealed solution

| Facet | Truth |
|---|---|
| **Identity** | **Mrs. Lillian Voss** — wheelchair user; genuinely limited most of the time; **not** “never disabled” |
| **Method** | She **engineers an apparent accident aimed at his predictable behavior**. She does not wrestle him. She prepares a hazard on a path or routine **he** will take alone (cellar stair he always uses, garage/workshop he locks himself into, beverage/medicine he always takes, door/timer/equipment he always operates). When he follows his own habit, the prepared failure kills him. She is nowhere that looks like “the scene of a fight.” |
| **Motive** | Henry was about to expose or remove her (theft from the house/trust, will/codicil, forced relocation). She must silence him **before** he talks to the family or solicitor. |
| **Summary** | Lillian wins with prep and underestimation. Henry dies doing something *for himself* (or for the house in his role), not serving her in the moment of death. Witnesses assume she could not reach, alter, or flee related objects. |

### How she acts without “he was helping me”

Combine as needed (all consistent with **no help-request kill**):

| Approach | How it works here |
|---|---|
| **His own behavior against him** | She knows he drinks the port in the study at 10; takes a specific tonic; always checks the cellar fuse after storms; always uses the back stair. Prep waits on **his** routine. |
| **Lure + trap (not “help me”)** | She gets him somewhere with a **pretext that is not assistance to her body** — e.g. “there’s a draft in the cellar,” “the dog is shut in,” “Henry, the accounts box is in the garage” (his job), “someone left the workshop light on.” He goes **as the man of the house / manager**, not as her arms and legs. She traps or times the hazard for when he’s inside. |
| **Remote / delayed prep** | Timer, propped door, sabotaged catch set **earlier**; she is in another room in her chair when he dies. |
| **Unwitting third party** | Maid moves a box or unlocks a door “because Mrs. Voss said Henry wanted it” — completes the chain without Lillian at the body. |
| **More mobile than assumed** | She can stand/take a few painful steps **earlier** to set the trap — not a full ambush at death time. |
| **Chair hides evidence** | Cushion / pouch / bay holds tool, pin, cord, note — link to prep. |

**Primary recommended package for this kernel:**

> **Routine + delayed hazard.** Henry always does X alone. Lillian prepared X to kill. She is elsewhere (or “helpless” in the drawing room) when he dies. Optional: a non-help lure that sends him into X as part of *his* duties.

### Capability truth

Witnesses wrongly equate disability with **zero agency**.  
She needs the chair for real life; she can still **plan**, **prep earlier**, use a **grabber/cord/attachment**, and/or take **a few steps with pain** during setup — not a miracle recovery fight scene.

## False appearance

1. Solitary accident — he was alone when it happened.  
2. Lillian couldn’t attack him.  
3. Lillian couldn’t reach the mechanism / shelf / latch.  
4. Lillian couldn’t leave a scene quickly — so she “couldn’t be involved.”  
5. Maybe blame another able-bodied relative who argued with Henry.

**Double-duty:** His known routine (early: character color; later: the weaponized schedule).

**Best physical tells (keep):**

- Shoe scuffs / brief stand marks (setup time, not “she ran”)  
- Seated-height tool placement  
- Unexpected wheelchair tracks (she was at the hazard earlier)  
- Hazard tuned to **his** height/habits  
- Item in the chair linking to prep  
- Trap/lure language that is about **the house/his job**, not “help me reach that”

## Solution graph

### Lead: `not-simple-accident`

- **Question:** Was Henry’s death a simple accident?
- **Claim:** No — the hazard was prepared for him.
- **Supports:** sabotage marks; failure mode that isn’t natural wear; calibrated to his height/habits  
- **minSupports:** 1  

### Lead: `his-routine-was-known`

- **Question:** Was someone counting on what Henry always does?
- **Claim:** Yes — the death sits on his predictable path/habit, not a random slip.
- **Supports:** testimony of routine; schedule; “he always…”; physical setup only works if he does the usual thing  
- **minSupports:** 1  

### Lead: `underestimation`

- **Question:** Could Lillian have arranged this without overpowering him?
- **Claim:** Yes — prep, tools, and/or limited mobility for setup; not a fight.
- **Supports:** tracks at hazard earlier; shoe/stand marks; grabber/tool; chair compartment; medical “brief stand possible”  
- **minSupports:** 2  

### Lead: `prep-window`

- **Question:** Who could have prepared the hazard before he walked into it?
- **Claim:** Lillian had access and solitude in the prep window; key alternatives alibied.
- **Supports:** staff timeline; tracks; others elsewhere  
- **minSupports:** 1  

### Lead: `motive-silence`

- **Question:** Why did Henry have to die this week?
- **Claim:** He was about to expose/remove Lillian.
- **Supports:** ledger/codicil; “he meant to speak to the solicitor”; her dependency on staying  
- **minSupports:** 2

## One-paragraph pitch (corrected)

> Henry Cole dies alone at Greymoor in what looks like bad luck on a path he always takes. He was not helping anyone when he died. Mrs. Lillian Voss, who uses a wheelchair, had prepared that path and counted on his habits — and on everyone assuming she could not plan or reach what killed him. She never needs to overpower him. She needs him to be predictable, and the house to underestimate her. You prove the accident was built and that the person in the chair is the one who built it.
