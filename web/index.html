The Pier at Low Tide

You are in the harbormaster’s office

A desk lamp still burns. Charts curl on the wall. Through the salt-streaked window you can see the black water where they pulled him out. The door to the pier stands half open; cold air slides in with the smell of diesel and low tide.

You I’m going out onto the pier.

Wet planks under your shoes. Rope, cold metal, the slap of water against the pilings. Mara leans against a post, smoking. Old Tom sits on an overturned crate, eyes anywhere but the water. Near the edge of the dock, a dark stain hasn’t quite rinsed clean.

Your message

I want a closer look at that stain on the dock…

You are on the pier

You I kneel and check the dark stain on the edge of the dock.

Not rust. Darker — sticky where the wood hasn’t fully dried. Someone tried to scrub it in a hurry and missed the groove between the boards.

You What’s under the ladder for the skiff?

Wedged in a rung: a crumpled receipt, still damp. Pier Locker 3 · 4:12 a.m. · paid cash.

You pocket the receipt. It’s in your evidence now.

Your message

Mara, I need to ask you something…

With Mara · on the pier

You Where were you when he died?
Mara Home. Asleep. Ask anyone on Dock Three — they saw my lights out.
You Then explain this. I found a receipt for locker 3 at 4:12 a.m. That’s your locker, isn’t it?
Mara She laughs once, thin. “Plenty of people use those lockers.”
You Number 3 is yours. You said you were home.
Mara The cigarette burns down to the filter. She doesn’t look at the water. “Fine. I was here. But I didn’t kill him.”

Her alibi doesn’t hold. She admits she was on the pier.

Your message

Why were you really here that early?

Closing the case when you’re ready

You’ve walked the pier, broken an alibi, and the receipt still smells like the sea. When you’re sure, you say so.

You I know who did it. Mara killed the harbormaster. She was here at low tide, struck him on the dock, tried to scrub the stain, and needed him quiet about the night runs.

You lay it out: the stain between the boards, locker 3 at 4:12, the alibi that only held until the paper came out of your pocket. Mara doesn’t run. She looks at the black water and nods once, small and tired.

“He was going to sink the whole crew,” she says. “I only meant to stop him talking. The rail was wet. He went over before I could—” Her voice fails. Old Tom turns away. Somewhere inland, a siren starts the long road down to the harbor.

Mara Voss — manslaughter on the pier at low tide. The harbormaster’s ledger will name the night runs. The town will pretend it never knew. You close your notebook. The case is finished.
